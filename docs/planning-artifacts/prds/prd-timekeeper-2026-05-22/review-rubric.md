# PRD Quality Review — TimeKeeper

## Overall verdict

This is a strong, unusually disciplined PRD for what it is: a Fast-path/headless hackathon spec for an internal Harvest clone whose differentiator is a first-class MCP server. It has a real thesis ("Harvest has no event layer and no agent interface; TimeKeeper inverts that"), feature prioritization that follows from it, FRs with genuinely testable consequences, honest de-scoping with a tight Non-Goals section, and counter-metrics that guard against the obvious hackathon failure modes (tool padding, breadth chasing, event noise). What's at risk is mostly downstream-mechanical and consciously-deferred: a handful of `[NOTE FOR PM]` callouts live in §9.2 rather than at the FRs they tension (the FR-3/FR-6 approval-lock dependency is the sharpest), the demo-critical Open Questions (OQ-5, OQ-7) are still genuinely open with no recommended fallback that the build sequence honors, and FR-22's "stretch" status contradicts its §9.2 "Should" classification. None of these are blockers for a hackathon green-light; they are the residue of skipping interactive discovery, which is expected and disclosed.

## Decision-readiness — strong

A decision-maker can act on this. Decisions are stated as decisions, not smuggled in as "considerations." §6 Constraints commits to single-tenant, static-bearer auth, and a ~2-day time-box without hedging; §8 Non-Goals draws the billing line as "the single largest deferral and the clearest scope line" (§8, first bullet) rather than soft-pedaling it. Trade-offs name what was given up: §E in the addendum lists six rejected/deferred alternatives each with a one-line cost ("reimplementing Harvest's REST contract adds work with no demo value"). The Open Questions in §11 are mostly real — OQ-5 (is a human web UI required for the demo?) and OQ-7 (local vs. Azure deploy) are live and consequential.

Where it slips: several Open Questions are rhetorical — they pose the question and answer it in the same line (OQ-2 "duration + Timer only (recommended)", OQ-3 "single-tenant (recommended)", OQ-4 "static token (recommended)"). That's defensible for a Fast-path PRD recording an inferred default, but per the rubric's "not rhetorical questions with an answer in the next sentence" test, these are decisions already made and parked in §6/§8, not open questions. They'd be better framed as `[ASSUMPTION]` + a confirm prompt. The genuinely open ones (OQ-5, OQ-7) are the decision-relevant ones and they are honestly open.

### Findings
- **medium** Demo-critical OQs lack a load-bearing fallback the build plan commits to (§11 OQ-5, OQ-7) — the demo's shape (web UI required? deployed where?) is unresolved, yet SM-1/SM-2 assume a runnable end-to-end demo and §9.2 says "the demo can run through an MCP client + Inspector." The PRD recommends a fallback but doesn't *commit* one, so "done" for the headline metric floats. *Fix:* promote the §9.2 "agent + Inspector suffices" stance to a stated default in §6, and have the build sequence step 6 (addendum §F) name the demo target explicitly so OQ-5/OQ-7 become confirmations, not blockers.
- **low** Rhetorical Open Questions (§11 OQ-2, OQ-3, OQ-4) — these are decisions already taken in §6/§8 with "(recommended)" answers inline; listing them as open dilutes the signal of the genuinely open OQ-5/OQ-7. *Fix:* recharacterize OQ-2/3/4 as confirmed assumptions (they already appear in §6/§8/§12) and reserve §11 for items without a chosen default.

## Substance over theater — strong

Little furniture here. The Vision (§1) is product-specific and falsifiable — it makes a concrete claim about Harvest ("no event layer and no agent interface — integrators can only poll its REST API") that could be wrong and that the whole product is a bet against. Swap it into another PRD and it breaks; that is the anti-theater test passing.

Personas (§2.1) are load-bearing: each shapes a visible scope decision. Member's "sees only their own time" becomes FR-13's Member rule; PM's "sees their managed projects' data; not the whole account" becomes FR-13's PM rule and FR-17's filter-scoping consequence; AI Agent's "peer interface, not a bridge" becomes NFR-4 and the whole §4.7. There are four personas — at the rubric's stated ceiling, not over it — and §2.3 Non-Users does real work by explicitly excluding clients/finance/build-time agents. The build-time vs. run-time agent distinction (§0, §2.1, §8) is the single most valuable disambiguation in the document and is not theater.

NFRs (§5) carry product-specific thresholds, not boilerplate: NFR-6 commits to "~500 ms p95" and immediately disclaims it as "a demo-quality target, not a production SLA" — honest, bounded, not aspirational. NFR-1's "decimal/numeric, never floating point" is a specific correctness commitment traceable to a real Harvest schema gotcha (addendum §C: "weekly_capacity is in seconds in Harvest"). The innovation claim (event-native + MCP-native) is the actual differentiator, not a template-mandated section.

### Findings
- **low** NFR-5 ("Events as the observability backbone") is the thinnest NFR — it asserts events "double as the activity/audit trail" but sets no bound (retention, completeness, queryability) the way NFR-1/NFR-6 do. *Fix:* either give it a testable floor (e.g. "every state-changing operation produces a queryable event with actor + timestamp") or fold it into FR-21's consequences, where the testable list already lives.

## Strategic coherence — strong

The PRD has a thesis and bets on it. The arc — rebuild only Harvest's daily-use core (log → watch budget → report), make it operable by an agent as a first-class user — is stated in §1 and every feature block serves it. Prioritization follows the thesis, not ease: §9.1/§9.2 and the addendum §F build sequence both put the agent loop (FR-23–FR-25) ahead of governance (FR-15/16) and reminders (FR-22), with the explicit rule "favor a working signature demo (UJ-5) over breadth" (§6). UJ-5 is named the signature demo in three places (§2.4, §4.7, SM-1) and the metrics validate the thesis rather than measuring activity: SM-1 is end-to-end agent success, SM-4 is permission parity across interfaces (validating the "peer not bridge" claim), SM-5 is tool self-sufficiency. Counter-metrics exist and are pointed (SM-C1 "tool count is not a goal," SM-C3 "event volume is not value") — exactly the discipline the rubric asks for when SMs are present. The MVP scope kind is coherently "problem-solving + platform" (a working core plus an agent surface), and the scope logic matches.

- (none — this dimension holds.)

## Done-ness clarity — strong

This is the dimension story creation leans on hardest, and it is the PRD's strongest. Every FR (FR-1 through FR-26) carries an explicit **Consequences (testable)** block, and most consequences are genuinely verifiable conditions, not adjectives. Examples that would survive a skeptical engineer: FR-1 "if the browser closes with a Timer running, the Timer is still running on next login and shows correct elapsed time" and "stopped after under one minute still produces a valid entry (hours ≥ 0)"; FR-20 "It fires once per crossing, not on every subsequent entry while still over" plus the re-arm condition; FR-3 "Entering 0 (or clearing) a cell that has an entry deletes that entry." The SMs reinforce done-ness with pass/fail targets ("3/3 modes correct," "0 errors on the happy path," "exactly-once across a scripted sequence").

I found no instances of the banned vagueness ("handles X gracefully," "reasonable performance," "user-friendly") in any FR consequence — a notable result for a PRD this size. The few soft spots are scoped and flagged: FR-22's "expectation = at least one entry" is tagged `[ASSUMPTION]` for refinement, and FR-18's "within 60 seconds (or live)" is a defensible demo-grade bound.

### Findings
- **low** FR-23 leans on out-of-file detail for its full done-ness (§4.7 FR-23) — the testable consequence says the tool set covers a list "at minimum for MVP" but defers the full catalogue to `addendum.md`; an engineer needs both files open to know what "done" is for the MCP surface. This is acceptable given the PRD/addendum split is deliberate, but the MVP subset boundary is fuzzier than the rest. *Fix:* in addendum §B.1 the "MVP?" column already disambiguates per-tool — cross-reference it explicitly from FR-23 ("MVP subset = addendum §B.1 'MVP?' = Yes") so the boundary is single-sourced.
- **low** FR-21 "enough identity to act on it" (§4.6 FR-21) is the one near-adjective in the consequences — "enough" is judgment, not a bound, though the parenthetical ("entity id(s), acting user, timestamp") rescues it. *Fix:* drop "enough identity to act on it" and lead with the concrete payload list as the requirement.

## Scope honesty — strong (with one structural gap)

Omissions are explicit, not inferred. §8 Non-Goals does real work and names the billing cluster as the clearest scope line; `[NON-GOAL for MVP]` callouts appear inline at the points of temptation (FR-8 rate modes, FR-22 reminders, FR-26 notifications) and are consolidated in §9.2. Eight `[ASSUMPTION]` tags carry inferences the headless run couldn't confirm, and all eight are collected in §12 — the roundtrip holds (see Mechanical notes). De-scoping is proposed openly with Should/Stretch labels and a "deferred, not abandoned" framing (§9.2 header). For a Fast-path PRD, the open-items density (8 OQs + 8 assumptions + 2 PM notes) is appropriate to the stakes — high counts are fine on a hackathon PRD per the rubric, and they are disclosed rather than hidden.

The one structural gap is a `[NOTE FOR PM]` placement problem. The rubric wants PM notes "at real tensions, not at safe checkpoints." The sharpest tension in the document — that FR-3 and FR-6 both encode an approval-lock behavior that depends on FR-15/FR-16, which are themselves deferred to §9.2 — is flagged, but the note lives in §9.2 ("if approval is cut, those lock behaviors become inert but should remain coded") rather than at FR-3/FR-6 where a reader first hits the dependency. A reader working FR-3 in isolation (exactly what story creation does) sees "a cell whose entry has Approval Status approved is read-only" with no signal that the producer of that status is out of MVP scope.

### Findings
- **medium** Approval-lock dependency note is downstream of the dependency (§9.2 vs. §4.1 FR-3 / §4.1 FR-6) — FR-3 and FR-6 require an `approved` status that only FR-15/FR-16 (deferred) can produce; the only callout warning of this lives in §9.2. A story author pulling FR-3 alone won't see it. *Fix:* add a one-line `[NOTE FOR PM]` or inline pointer at FR-3 and FR-6 ("`approved` status is produced by FR-16, deferred per §9.2; lock code should remain even if approval is cut").
- **low** FR-22 status conflict — scope-honesty signal is contradictory (§4.6 FR-22 vs. §9.2): the inline tag reads `[NON-GOAL for MVP — Should]` while §9.2 lists it under "Should" but the §4.6 description and §9.2 both call it not-on-demo-path; meanwhile FR-26 is consistently "Stretch." The Should/Stretch labeling is fine; the issue is FR-22 carries both a NON-GOAL tag and a Should label in the same line, which reads as a category error. *Fix:* standardize the inline convention — `[NON-GOAL for MVP — Should]` vs. `[NON-GOAL for MVP — stretch]` (FR-26) is inconsistent in casing and concept; pick one label vocabulary.

## Downstream usability — strong

This PRD is explicitly chain-top (the closing line lists `bmad-create-ux-design`, `bmad-create-architecture`, `bmad-create-epics-and-stories` as consumers), so this dimension matters and the PRD is built for it. A Glossary (§3) is present and the document self-imposes verbatim usage ("FRs, UJs, and SMs use them verbatim; no synonyms appear elsewhere"). FR/UJ/SM IDs are contiguous and unique (FR-1..FR-26, UJ-1..UJ-5, SM-1..SM-5 + SM-C1..SM-C3 — see Mechanical notes for the full audit). Cross-references resolve: FR consequences cite FR-13 for permission scoping repeatedly and the target exists; UJs are referenced inline from features ("Realizes UJ-3, UJ-4") and every UJ named resolves to §2.4. Each feature block is pullable alone because it opens with a Description and references other requirements by ID/Glossary term rather than "see above." The PRD/addendum split is clean: §7 summarizes the MCP surface and points to addendum §A–§B for the authoritative catalogue, and the addendum names itself the primary input for `bmad-create-architecture`.

### Findings
- **low** Glossary defines two terms the body never uses as defined nouns — **Timesheet** is defined (§3) and used (FR-15/16), good; but **Spent Date**, **Weekly Capacity**, **Approval Status** are used heavily while a couple of Glossary entries (e.g. the parenthetical distinction in **Access Role** between account-level PM and per-Project PM flag) are subtle enough that downstream may conflate them. Not drift, but a known confusion surface. *Fix:* none required for build; flag the Access-Role/PM-flag duality for the UX and stories workflows since it governs FR-13/FR-14 enforcement.

## Shape fit — strong

The PRD correctly identifies its own shape and resists over-formalizing. Per the rubric, an internal single-operator tool can be capability-spec-shaped with UJs as potential overhead — but TimeKeeper is *multi-role* internal (Member/PM/Admin/Agent), so UJs and personas are genuinely load-bearing here, not ceremony. The five UJs map cleanly to the four human roles plus the agent, and UJ-5 (the agent journey) is the one that justifies the whole project — so UJ density is earned, not padded. SMs are correctly operational/demo-oriented ("demonstrated live," "3/3 modes correct") rather than forced into consumer-product engagement metrics — exactly right for a hackathon internal tool, and the PRD says so (§10 preamble). The brownfield-adjacent risk (this rebuilds Harvest) is handled honestly: the addendum reuses Harvest's data model as a baseline but explicitly rejects mirroring its REST contract (§E, OQ-8), and Harvest-specific behaviors are tagged as assumptions (>24h/day, weekly_capacity-in-seconds, show-budget-to-all) rather than asserted as TimeKeeper fact.

If anything, the PRD is at the *upper* edge of formalization for a 2-day build (full Consequences blocks on all 26 FRs, a separate addendum, an Assumptions Index). For this rubric that is a feature, not a fault — substance bar is met and the rigor pays off downstream — but a leaner team could have shipped with fewer FRs. No under-formalization anywhere.
